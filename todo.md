## Sub-Optimal Technology

> How can a society be look-in? How can we get into an state of the configuration of the social system;  which is hard ‚Ä¶?
> 

**1. Path dependence and suboptimal lock-in**

- **Definition:** Once a society adopts a technology or system, it can be ‚Äúlocked in‚Äù even if better alternatives exist, because switching is costly, disruptive, or requires coordination.
- **Mechanism:** Early choices create networks, habits, and complementary systems that make the initial technology persist.

**Examples:**

- **QWERTY keyboard:** Not the most efficient layout, but widespread adoption locked it in.
- **Internal combustion engines:** Oil-based infrastructure reinforced decades of fossil-fuel dependence.
- **BNPL systems like Klarna:** Exploit current payment frictions and credit norms. Without fully digital, programmable money, the system persists even if a better monetary infrastructure could eliminate the need.

**2. Why it matters**

- Societies may continue **suboptimal consumption patterns** or **inefficient technologies** because of network effects, habits, and institutional inertia.
- These ‚Äúpatches‚Äù (like Klarna) **solve symptoms, not the underlying problem** ‚Äî but they reinforce the old system, making it harder to adopt the optimal solution (fully digital, programmable money).

**3. Systems-theoretic perspective**

- **State:** Society stuck with suboptimal tech.
- **Feedback loops:** Convenience, short-term profit, and social habit reinforce the technology.
- **Barrier to improvement:** Transitioning to the optimal system is costly or requires collective action, so the patch survives.

So in a **production chain**, the stage that captures the most value is typically where:

[

\text{Value Captured} \approx \text{Market Size} \times \text{Product Complexity}

]

- **High market size √ó low complexity:** Lots of sales, but margins might be low (commodities).
- **Low market size √ó high complexity:** Niche, specialized products; high margins, but limited volume.
- **High market size √ó high complexity:** Sweet spot ‚Äî mass-market but hard-to-produce goods; maximum value capture.

This framework is why, for example, **software or AI models** capture more value than raw materials: they are both complex and scalable to large markets.
